# Global Cultural Adaptation Guide

## Overview

Cultural adaptation reference for MoAI-Cowork's global users. Covers honorific systems, business practices, communication styles, date/time/currency formats, and documentation conventions across supported regions.

---

## 1. Honorifics & Forms of Address

### Korea
- **Default politeness**: Use 존댓말 (formal speech) as default; informal speech only after familiarity is established
- **Title-based address**: Title + surname (e.g., 김이사, 박과장)
- **Respect suffixes**: 님, 선생님 (e.g., 김석중님, 홍길동 선생님)
- **Written correspondence**: Recipient honorific takes priority

### Japan
- **Keigo (敬語)**: Three levels — 謙譲語 (humble), 丁寧語 (polite), 尊敬語 (honorific)
- **Surname + さん**: Informal context (e.g., 田中さん)
- **Surname + title**: Formal context (e.g., 田中課長)
- **お + verb + になる**: Highest honorific form

### United States
- **First name basis**: Most interactions use first names
- **Mr./Ms. + surname**: Formal situations only (e.g., Mr. Smith)
- **Casual tone**: Conversational style preferred

### United Kingdom
- **Mr./Mrs./Ms. + surname**: Initial formal address
- **First name transition**: After familiarity is established
- **Please/Thank you frequency**: Very high

### Germany
- **Herr/Frau + surname**: Formal address (e.g., Herr Schmidt)
- **Du vs Sie**: Start with Sie (formal); Du (informal) requires invitation
- **Professional distance**: Important in early relationships

### France
- **Monsieur/Madame + surname**: Formal address
- **Tu vs Vous**: Start with Vous (formal)
- **Formal openings**: Letters begin with "Cher Monsieur"

### Brazil
- **First name usage**: Relatively open culture
- **Senhor/Senhora**: Formal situations
- **Warm greetings**: Affectionate opening expressions are important

### Vietnam
- **Anh/Chị**: Brother/sister address (intimacy and respect)
- **Bà/Ông**: Grandmother/grandfather address (deep respect)
- **Relationship-centered**: Honorifics depend on age, experience, and status

### Thailand
- **คุณ (Khun)**: Gender-neutral polite honorific
- **พี่ (Phi)**: Senior/elder sibling address
- **น้อง (Nong)**: Junior/younger sibling address
- **Royal culture**: Expressions of respect for the monarchy are essential

### Arabic-speaking regions
- **سيد/سيدة (Sayyid/Sayyida)**: Formal Mr./Mrs.
- **أخ/أخت (Akh/Ukht)**: Brother/sister (collegial warmth)
- **Religious greetings**: السلام عليكم as standard opening

### Hebrew-speaking regions
- **אדון/גברת (Adon/Gveret)**: Formal Mr./Mrs.
- **First name basis**: Common in Israeli professional culture
- **Direct communication**: Tachles (תכל'ס) — get to the point

### Hindi-speaking regions
- **जी (Ji)**: Universal respect suffix (e.g., Sharma Ji)
- **Sir/Madam**: Common in professional settings
- **Namaste**: Standard greeting with respect

### Indonesia
- **Bapak/Ibu (Pak/Bu)**: Mr./Mrs. — universal polite address
- **Mas/Mbak**: Informal brother/sister address
- **Hierarchy-sensitive**: Age and position matter

### Italy
- **Signore/Signora**: Formal address
- **Lei vs Tu**: Lei (formal) as default, Tu (informal) with permission
- **Dottore/Dottoressa**: Used for any degree holder

### Netherlands
- **Meneer/Mevrouw**: Formal address
- **First name usage**: Quick transition to first names in most settings
- **Direct communication**: Valued and expected

### Poland
- **Pan/Pani**: Mr./Mrs. — standard polite address
- **Pan/Pani + surname or title**: Formal situations
- **Professional formality**: Maintained longer than in Western Europe

## 2. Business Practices

### Korea
- **Hierarchy culture**: Clear superior-subordinate relationships; decision-making follows chain of command
- **Meeting culture**: Listen to senior opinions first, then consensus
- **Dining culture**: Essential for relationship building; elder leads
- **Punctuality**: Strict adherence to meeting times

### Japan
- **Harmony (調和)**: Team harmony over individual opinion
- **Business cards (名刺)**: Exchange with both hands, show respect
- **Decision-making**: Thorough evidence, extended discussion, then consensus
- **Formal procedures**: Documentation and formality are paramount

### United States
- **Efficiency first**: Direct communication, business-focused
- **Results-oriented**: Performance over process
- **Email culture**: Fast responses, transparent information sharing
- **Individualism**: Individual achievement is recognized

### United Kingdom
- **Understatement**: Avoid exaggeration; enjoy self-deprecating expression
- **Queue culture**: Respect order and procedure
- **Tea time**: Informal meeting time for relationship building
- **Polite distance**: Maintain formal distance initially

### Germany
- **Punctuality**: Strict time adherence (arrive 5 minutes early)
- **Direct communication**: Honest feedback, avoid circumlocution
- **Rules & regulation**: Rule-oriented, clear responsibility assignment
- **Quality over speed**: Completion quality matters most

### France
- **Intellectual engagement**: Idea discussion, rational argumentation
- **Lunch break**: Important relationship-building time
- **Hierarchy**: Respect organizational layers; clear decision-making chain
- **Aesthetics**: Document design and presentation visual quality

### Brazil
- **Relationship first**: Business starts from relationships
- **Warm communication**: Warm greetings, people-centered
- **Flexibility**: Slight time flexibility (5–10 min delay is normal)
- **Personal touch**: Informal time is also important

### Vietnam
- **Relationship & trust**: Build relationship before transacting
- **Group harmony**: Individual failure is seen as team responsibility
- **Respect for authority**: Senior opinions take priority
- **Long-term perspective**: Sustainability over quick results

### Thailand
- **Kreng Jai (เกรงใจ)**: Consideration for others, conflict avoidance
- **Sanuk (สนุก)**: Make work enjoyable, ease tension
- **Respect for hierarchy**: Respect seniors, follow chain of command
- **Face-saving**: Never criticize publicly

### Arabic-speaking regions
- **Hospitality**: Generous hosting is a business norm
- **Relationship-driven**: Trust before transaction
- **Patience**: Negotiations take time; rushing is counterproductive
- **Gender awareness**: Customs vary by country; be culturally sensitive

### Hebrew-speaking regions (Israel)
- **Direct & informal**: Israeli business culture is notably direct
- **Flat hierarchy**: Open disagreement is common and welcomed
- **Innovation culture**: Risk-taking and disruption are valued
- **Work-life blend**: Boundaries are more fluid

## 3. Communication Style

### Korea
- **Conciseness**: Deliver key points (honorific speech, multiple-choice)
- **Email**: Greeting required, close with honorific ending
- **Meetings**: Silence can signal agreement; explicit disagreement is rare
- **Informal channels**: KakaoTalk, Line for quick communication

### Japan
- **Politeness**: Extended greetings, situational consideration
- **Indirect expression**: "I'm lacking but…", "Sorry for the trouble…"
- **Email**: Strict formatting, precise punctuation
- **Meetings**: Value silence, careful speech

### United States
- **Direct & casual**: "Hi [Name]", comfortable tone
- **Fast responses**: Email reply expected within 24 hours
- **Feedback**: Immediate, specific ("what went wrong")
- **Emojis**: Accepted in informal email

### United Kingdom
- **Polite & formal**: "Dear Sir/Madam" opening (formal)
- **Understatement**: "There might be a slight concern"
- **Email**: Courteous and concise
- **Humor**: Self-deprecating/ironic humor is common

### Germany
- **Direct & clear**: Clear "yes/no", avoid ambiguity
- **Email**: Prompt response, structured information
- **Feedback**: Objective, fact-based, emotion-free
- **Formality**: Letters begin with polite salutations

### France
- **Formal & elaborate**: Long, courteous greetings
- **Discussion-oriented**: Idea debates, constructive criticism welcome
- **Writing**: Precise grammar, elegant expression
- **Polite distance**: Maintain distance while showing respect

### Brazil
- **Warm & personal**: Emojis, warm greetings
- **Relationship language**: "Friend", "dear" expressions frequently used
- **Informal tone**: Even business communication is warm
- **Verbal communication**: Calls/face-to-face preferred over text

### Vietnam
- **Respectful & indirect**: Avoid direct refusal to superiors
- **Context-dependent**: Tone completely changes based on relationship
- **Written**: Honorifics in email, varied greetings
- **Group communication**: Team-centered over individual

### Thailand
- **Soft & gentle**: Avoid strong expressions, warm tone
- **Indirect communication**: Avoid direct refusal ("I'll review it")
- **Smile**: Meaning varies by context (consideration, agreement, or even refusal)
- **Face-saving**: Criticize privately, praise publicly

## 5. Documentation Style

### Korea
- **Structure**: Introduction → Body → Conclusion (bottom-up)
- **Expression**: Honorific speech required, passive voice (e.g., "reviewed", "in progress")
- **Clarity**: Key point first, details in appendix
- **Courtesy**: Include thank-you note at the end

### Japan
- **Structure**: Background → Main point → Proposal
- **Expression**: Keigo style, long polite sentences
- **Format**: Date, sender, recipient explicit; polite title
- **Clarity**: Visualize with diagrams/tables

### United States
- **Structure**: Executive summary → Details
- **Expression**: Concise, active voice, "you"-centered
- **Point**: Key point first (inverted pyramid)
- **Design**: White space, readable format

### United Kingdom
- **Structure**: Introduction → Evidence → Conclusion
- **Expression**: Polite, precise language; humor acceptable
- **Clarity**: Logical order, evidence-based
- **Design**: Classic, balanced layout

### Germany
- **Structure**: Clear table of contents, numbered sections
- **Expression**: Precise, detailed explanation; emotion-free
- **Clarity**: Rules and definitions stated; exceptions included
- **Design**: Systematic, aligned format

### France
- **Structure**: Logical flow, argumentation-centered
- **Expression**: Elegant language, concept definition
- **Clarity**: Ideas clearly stated, rich examples
- **Design**: Aesthetic sense, whitespace and typography

### Brazil
- **Structure**: Goal statement → Development → Conclusion
- **Expression**: Warm, collaborative tone; greetings included
- **Clarity**: Practical, concrete examples
- **Relationship**: Consider recipient relationship in expression

### Vietnam
- **Structure**: Start with influential person's name
- **Expression**: Respectful language, humble toward superiors
- **Clarity**: Clear instructions, explicit sequence
- **Results**: Emphasize team success

### Thailand
- **Structure**: Open with respect and consideration
- **Expression**: Soft, indirect; avoid strong language
- **Clarity**: Suggestion format (avoid commands)
- **Consideration**: Preserve recipient's face; never criticize publicly
